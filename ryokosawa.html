About Me

    Work Experience

  1. [Virginia Tech - Graduate Research Assistant] I am currently leading a project focusing on developing an in vitro traumatic brain injury (TBI) model, specifically investigating glial cell (oligodendrocyte precursor cell, microglia, and astrocyte) responses to blast-induced injury. My responsibilities include coordinating regular team meetings to discuss experimental design and results, culturing glial cells in both 2D and 3D environments, conducting molecular and cellular analyses, and interpreting and reporting data. My technical expertise includes quantitative PCR, Western blotting, fluorescence and luminescence-based microplate assays, immunocytochemistry, confocal microscopy, and proteomics analysis. I have presented my research at five scientific conferences through posters and oral presentations and co-authored a peer-reviewed review article. I published a first-author research manuscript regarding the mechanosensing of OPC investigating a molecular mechanism following traumatic brain injury in March 2025. Currently, we are preparing a manuscript focusing on OPC's acute molecular responses following a high-rate mechanical insult using proteomic analysis that will be submitted in February 2026.

  2. [FDA - Researcher/Cell Biologist] In this position, I was primarily responsible for managing a liver-on-a-chip microfluidics system. My work involved planning and conducting drug testing experiments using the device, including operation, maintenance, and troubleshooting during experimental procedures. I cultured hepatocytes on the microfluidic platform and regularly reported experimental results and system performance to the research team. In addition to my primary role, I supported collaborative projects by assisting with the culture of induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) for cardiomyocyte differentiation and operating a microfluidic injection system. I contributed to the preparation of a research manuscript and a conference poster, and I co-authored two published research articles and presented a poster at an FDA research meeting.

  3. [AMED - Medical R&D Specialist] In this role, I supported international scientific collaboration by reporting on biomedical research funding trends in the U.S., particularly those related to the National Institutes of Health (NIH), to promote U.S.–Japan research partnerships. I regularly attended scientific meetings and conferences to stay informed on emerging research priorities across biomedical fields. I contributed to the planning and execution of U.S.–Japan collaboration events, including the Cherry Blossom Festival exhibit and the Human Frontier Science Program, which fostered networking among early career researchers. I conducted data analysis and authored reports using publicly available NIH resources, including NIH RePORTER and the All of Us Research Program. This work led to the publication of two publicly available reports in Japanese and the presentation of two posters at NIH–Japanese government collaborative events.
